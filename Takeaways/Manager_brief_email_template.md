# Manager Brief — Email Template

---

> **How to use this template:** Fill in every `[PLACEHOLDER]` with your own details. Read it back to yourself before sending — it should sound like you, not like a workshop handout. If a section does not fit your organisation or your relationship with your manager, cut it or rewrite it. The structure is a guide, not a script. Send it this week, while the conversation is easy to start.

## The Email

---

**Subject:** AI pilot proposal — following the Cisco workshop on [DATE]

---

**To:** [MANAGER NAME / LEADERSHIP TEAM]

**From:** [YOUR NAME]

---

[MANAGER NAME],

I attended the Cisco AI Technical Workshop in London on [DATE] and came away with something more useful than I expected: a working prototype, a concrete proposal, and a first draft of a governance framework we could actually use. I wanted to share a brief summary and put a specific idea on the table.

The day was built around three commitments that I think matter for how we approach this: no coding required at any point, free and open-source tools throughout, and an explicit design principle that AI augments human judgement rather than replacing it. Those are not marketing claims — they were the structural rules the entire day was built around, and they held.

In practical terms, I left with a local AI assistant running on my laptop, a working agentic workflow that reads our own internal documents and compares them against live web sources, and a first draft of an AI Acceptable Use Policy. None of it required a budget, a developer, or anything beyond a standard corporate laptop.

**The proposal:** I would like to run a 30-day internal pilot with a small group — [TEAM/FUNCTION, e.g. "two or three people from the network team"] — using the same tools and approach from the workshop. The pilot would involve no production systems, no sensitive data outside our existing controls, and no external cost. The goal is straightforward: test whether these tools save us meaningful time on [SPECIFIC TASK, e.g. "monthly firmware compliance reporting / IT policy review / service desk first-line triage"], document what works and what needs governance guardrails, and produce a short recommendation for whether and how to scale.

This is a low-risk starting point precisely because the tools stay local, the scope is narrow, and the output is a recommendation — not a deployment decision. It gives us direct evidence to work from rather than vendor briefings or secondhand reports.

**The ask:** Could we find 30 minutes [THIS WEEK / NEXT WEEK] to talk through the scope and decide whether it is worth proceeding? I have the workshop materials, the lab files, and a one-page agent brief I put together on the back of today that might be useful context.

## If You Get a "Not Now" Response

That is a useful answer too. The three most common objections and a brief response to each:

**"Our IT security team would never approve this."**
The tools used in the pilot run entirely on local machines — no data leaves the device during the local AI assistant work (Session 2). For the agentic workflow (Session 3), the data flow is fully transparent and documented. The AI Acceptable Use Policy draft from today gives the security team something specific to review and approve, rather than a vague request. Offer to share it as the starting point for that conversation.

**"We don't have the resource right now."**
The pilot does not require dedicated headcount. It is two to four people testing a tool on a task they are already doing, for 30 days. The time investment is hours, not weeks. The output is a written recommendation, not a deployment.

**"What's the business case?"**
The business case is time saved on a specific, measurable task. Identify one task your team currently does manually that involves reading documents, comparing them to external sources, or producing a structured written output. Run the agent on that task. Measure the time difference. That is the business case — built from your own numbers, not vendor projections.
